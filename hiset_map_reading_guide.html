Map Reading Primer

Maps account for roughly 10% of the Social Studies test on paper — but the skill of reading one correctly shows up everywhere else too. Five things to master, with practice questions at the end.

5 core skills Sample maps, not memorization 13 practice questions
How HiSET actually tests this
  • You won't be asked to name capitals from memory. Most map questions show an unfamiliar map — often invented just for the test — and ask you to read it correctly.
  • Almost every question tests one of two things: can you find a fact the map literally states, or can you draw a conclusion the map actually supports.
  • Wrong answers are almost always reasonable-sounding statements the map doesn't actually prove. Catching that gap is the real skill being measured.

Test tip: Latitude lines run horizontally, like rungs of a ladder, and measure how far north or south you are. Longitude lines run vertically and measure how far east or west you are. A single point can sit in two hemispheres at once — 40°N, 90°W is in both the Northern and Western Hemispheres.

Test tip: On a shaded (choropleth) map, the legend tells you what each shade level means — darker usually means "more," but confirm with the legend rather than assuming. Compare regions by their actual shade, not by how large they look on the page.
